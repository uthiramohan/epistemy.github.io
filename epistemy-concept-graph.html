Epistemy.AI · Context Graph Framework

The graph that designs better questions
also catches shallow answers

A context graph maps how ideas depend on each other. Three measurable signals — edge traversal, sequence coherence, and saliency ordering — separate genuine understanding from LLM-generated pattern matching.

The problem

Professors cannot reliably tell whether a student answer reflects genuine understanding or was produced by an LLM. Current detection tools analyze writing style. We propose analyzing reasoning structure instead.

The key observation: a well-understood concept is not an isolated fact — it sits in a web of dependencies. A student who truly understands cash flow statements does not just know the answer; they can trace the causal chain through accrual accounting, revenue recognition, and working capital changes.

Three signals from one graph

Every course has an implicit graph of concepts and their dependencies. Formalizing this graph lets us measure three distinct signals in any student response:

Signal I
Edge traversal
How many conceptual edges does the answer traverse? Too few signals skipped reasoning.
Signal II
Traversal sequence
Does the answer hit intermediate nodes in causally coherent order?
Signal III
Saliency ordering
Does the answer lead with the most relevant concept, or bury it in preamble?

The saliency insight

The third signal draws on a fundamental asymmetry between how humans and LLMs approach problems:

"They excel at breadth, and humans excel at depth, human experts at least. I think they're very complementary. But our current way of doing math and science is focused on depth because that's where human expertise is, because humans can't do breadth."
— Terence Tao, Dwarkesh Podcast, March 2026

Applied to our context graph: a human expert answering a question goes depth-first — straight to the most salient node, then builds outward along the critical path. An LLM goes breadth-first — it activates many adjacent nodes because they are statistically associated in training data, not because they are on the causal path of the argument.

This difference is measurable and resistant to prompt engineering. You can tell an LLM to be concise, but you cannot teach it which concept matters most for a specific question in a specific course context.

What we would need to validate

This framework makes three testable empirical claims:

Claim I: Edge traversal count in a context graph correlates with item discrimination index on real exams.

Claim II: The traversal gap between human and LLM answers — edges activated, sequence coherence — is measurable and consistent across question types.

Claim III: Saliency position (rank of the most relevant concept node in the answer) differs systematically between human expert answers and LLM-generated answers, even when both reach the same conclusion.

The next step is a controlled study: take a single course, build its context graph, score existing exam questions by edge count, and compare against actual student performance data and LLM-generated answers on the same questions — measuring all three signals.
